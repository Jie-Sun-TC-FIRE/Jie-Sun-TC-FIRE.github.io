Wildland Fire - Plume Dynamics

My research investigates the multi-scale processes governing wildland fire generated plume behavior, smoke transport, and firebrands caused sportting in complex terrain and various atmospheric conditions. I use numerical models and radar observations to understand the coupling between fire-induced flows and ambient atmospheric conditions.

Tropical Cyclone Intensity-Size Relation and Forecast

Tropical cyclone (TC) intensity and size are among the key factors that determines the severity of its hazard. However, the forcaste skill of both intensity and size needs to be improved. We introduced a new radially invariant to describe the relation between TC intensity and size, which accounts the angular momentum of TC and its loss. Instead of angular momentum, the new variable,

effective absolute angular momentum (eAAM)

keeps radially invariant for a steady state TC. In the eAAM model, TC intensity and size are linearly related. And more importantly, with any given TC wind speed and radius information, tipically at several m/s wind speed that is easily obtained by observation, the inside information of TC including intensity and size can be derived. We use this as experimental real-time hurricane intensity and size predictions for up coming TCs. The past forecast and current TC intensity-size forcast can be found from : https://amccao.wixsite.com/hurricanewindprofile Currently, I am studying how environmental factors such as vertical wind shear, ocean heat content, and inner-core processes influence AAM loss parameters in the eAAM model and improve the forecast of TC intensity and size for each individual TC under different environments.
